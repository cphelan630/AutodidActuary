The Probabilistic Mindset: Beyond Actuarial Exams

One of the most valuable skills developed through actuarial training is thinking probabilistically. While this is essential for pricing insurance products and estimating reserves, I've found its applications extend far beyond professional contexts.

When we think deterministically ("this will happen" or "this won't happen"), we're setting ourselves up for poor decision-making. Embracing uncertaintyâ€”assigning probabilities rather than binary outcomesâ€”leads to more nuanced thinking and better decisions.

For example, when deciding whether to pursue additional certifications beyond my actuarial designation, I've been weighing:

  • The probability of using those specific skills (60-70%)
  • The likelihood of alternative paths becoming more valuable (30-40%)
  • The distribution of potential salary increases (mean +15%, with high variance)
  • The probability of enjoying the material enough to complete it (75%)

This probabilistic approach helps me avoid the trap of false certainty that plagues many career decisions. It's a mental model I wish was taught more widely outside technical fields.

Learning in Public: Benefits and Challenges

I've recently embraced the concept of "learning in public"â€”sharing my learning process rather than just the polished results. This website is part of that commitment, and the experience has been transformative.

The benefits have been substantial:

  • Deeper understanding: Explaining concepts forces clarity
  • Accountability: Public commitments drive consistency
  • Unexpected connections: Other learners offer perspectives I wouldn't have considered
  • Creating value while learning: My notes help others on similar paths

However, there are challenges too. The fear of being wrong publicly is real, especially in technical fields like actuarial science where precision matters. I've had to develop comfort with saying "I'm still figuring this out" and "I might be wrong about this."

On balance, learning in public has accelerated my progress more than any other single change to my study methods. If you're pursuing self-directed learning in any field, I highly recommend giving it a try, even on a small scale.
